Introduction

The Constitution of India is the supreme law of India. It lays down the framework that defines the political principles, establishes the structure, procedures, powers, and duties of government institutions, and sets out fundamental rights, directive principles, and the duties of citizens.

Key Features

  • Parliamentary Form of Government:

    The Indian Constitution establishes a parliamentary system at both the central and state levels, where the executive is part of the legislature and accountable to it.

  • Fundamental Rights:

    The constitution guarantees fundamental rights to citizens, including the right to equality, freedom of speech, and protection against discrimination.

  • Directive Principles of State Policy (DPSPs):

    The DPSPs, found in Part IV of the constitution, outline the aims and objectives the government should strive for in governance, acting as a guide for welfare-oriented policies.

  • Single Citizenship:

    The constitution provides for a single citizenship for all Indians, irrespective of their domicile, promoting national unity and integration.

  • Federal System with Unitary Bias:

    The Indian constitution establishes a federal system, but with a strong central government, allowing the federation to transform into a unitary state in times of need.

  • Blend of Rigidity and Flexibility:

    The constitution allows for amendments, but some require a special majority, ensuring both the ability to adapt to changing circumstances and the preservation of core principles.

  • Integrated and Independent Judiciary:

    The constitution establishes an integrated and independent judiciary, ensuring the judiciary's freedom from executive and legislative influence.

  • Secularism:

    India is a secular state, as stated in the preamble, ensuring equal treatment of all religions.

  • Drawn from Various Sources:

    The Indian Constitution draws inspiration and provisions from various sources, including the Government of India Act of 1935, the British Constitution, the US Constitution, the Irish Constitution, and the Canadian Constitution.

  • Preamble:

    The preamble outlines the objectives and ideals of the constitution, serving as a guiding principle for governance and the protection of fundamental rights.

  • Independent Bodies:

    The constitution establishes independent bodies, such as the Election Commission and the Comptroller and Auditor General, to ensure the smooth functioning of democracy.

  • Separation of Powers:

    The constitution adheres to the principle of separation of powers, dividing authority among the legislative, executive, and judicial branches.

Historical Significance

The Constitution of India was adopted on 26th November 1949 and came into effect on 26th January 1950. It is the longest written national constitution in the world.The constitution declares India a sovereign, socialist, secular and democratic republic, assures its citizens justice, equality, and liberty, and endeavours to promote fraternity.The original 1950 constitution is preserved in a nitrogen-filled case at the Parliament Library Building in New Delhi.
